Sponsored Search
Job Market Paper

Abstract  Draft   Slides   Code  Data  BibTeX

 I develop a theory of digital marketplaces where an intermediary provides a platform for firms to list their product and where consumers engage in costly search to learn about the products. I find that when prices can be observed prior to costly search, a firm higher in the list charges a higher price and earns a larger profit. This result is in contrast with those from the standard models of prominence where prices are discovered after search. I augment this model by allowing the intermediary to determine, endogenously, the order in which products are displayed by conducting an auction. The effect of the auction on product prices is captured by the auction reserve price, which is chosen by the intermediary to maximise its commission from firms. I show that these commissions (paid per-click) enter the pricing equations as a fixed cost for the prominent firm while being analogous to marginal cost for the non-prominent firm.
